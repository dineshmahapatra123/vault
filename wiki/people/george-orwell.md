# George Orwell

**Role/Background:** British writer, journalist, and essayist (1903–1950). Born Eric Arthur Blair. Worked as an Imperial Police officer in Burma, then lived in poverty in Paris and London before becoming one of the 20th century's most influential political writers. Known for *Animal Farm* (1945) and *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949).

**Known For:** Making political writing into art. His prose is a model of clarity — direct, concrete, stripped of pretension. He believed good prose is "like a windowpane." He wrote *against* totalitarianism and *for* democratic socialism with unflinching consistency after 1936.

**Their Lens:** Orwell saw the world through the tension between aesthetic instinct and political obligation. He believed writers cannot be politically neutral — every aesthetic choice is also a political act. His lens was always: *what is the lie here, and what is the true fact?* He was suspicious of ideology but deeply committed to justice.

**Notable Pieces:**
- *Why I Write* (1946) — his essay on the four motives for writing; compiled into [[four-motives-for-writing]], [[political-writing-as-art]], [[prose-as-windowpane]]
- *Politics and the English Language* (1946) — language corruption as political tool; Orwell's six rules; compiled into [[language-and-political-corruption]], [[six-rules-of-clear-writing]]
- *Homage to Catalonia* — firsthand account of the Spanish Civil War
- *Animal Farm* — political allegory, first book fusing artistic and political purpose
- *Nineteen Eighty-Four* — totalitarianism, surveillance, the corruption of language
