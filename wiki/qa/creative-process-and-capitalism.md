# Q: What does the creative process owe to capitalism — or resist about it?

**Filed:** 2026-04-06
**Sources Consulted:** [[artist-as-truth-teller]], [[four-motives-for-writing]], [[spend-it-all]], [[writing-as-compulsion]], [[de-weirding-ai]], [[smart-stranger-editing]], [[zadie-smith]]

---

## Answer

Capitalism rewards the hoarded, the scalable, the safe. [[james-baldwin]] knew the hostility well: every society *"despises its artists while living and acclaims them when safely dead"* — once the mirror can be hung on a wall and called beautiful rather than dangerous. [[george-orwell]]'s egoism motive bends toward the market even as his deeper impulses resist it; the hunger to be remembered is capitalism's oldest offering. [[annie-dillard]]'s corrective is the most radical: spend it all now. The well refills. But capitalism insists on scarcity, on accumulation, on return. The creative process runs on opposite logic — abundance through expenditure.

The recent corporate adoption of AI reflects this same tension. As [[ethan-mollick]] argues, the dominant instinct is to [[de-weirding-ai]] — to strip the technology of its unpredictable, creative "weirdness" and turn it into a manageable tool for [[augmentation-vs-automation|simple automation]]. This is capitalism's immune response to a technology that, like the artist, demands experimentation and tolerates failure.

Zadie Smith identifies the **industrial collision** as the "cruel" point where the creative process meets the market. The "Proof" stage is where the fluid dream of the work meets the "cold reality" of the production cycle. Her "24-karat gold-plated advice"—to **[[smart-stranger-editing|step away from the vehicle]]**—is a direct act of economic resistance. It requires a writer to prioritize critical distance over immediate "speed back to market." For Smith, the very act of talking about "craft" to a public audience feels "fraudulent," as it converts a private, sentence-level language into a commodified academic product.
