# Indian Railways Modernisation

## What's the Story
Indian Railways — one of the world's largest rail networks (1,37,000+ km, 25,000 daily trains, 20 million daily passengers) — has been running a decade-long infrastructure overhaul since 2014. The transformation is primarily about track: renewal, faster testing, mechanised maintenance, and safety fencing. The headline result is a near-elimination of track-related accidents even as traffic has grown.

## Key Arguments
**For the modernisation narrative:**
- Numbers are hard to dispute: consequential accidents fell from 135 (2014-15) to 16 (2025-26); accident rate per million train-km improved 90%
- Speed capacity has genuinely shifted: 80% of the network now handles 110 kmph+, up from 40%
- The machine fleet doubling (748 → 1,785) represents real capital deployment, not just policy paper

**Complications worth holding:**
- The piece is written by the minister responsible — it is advocacy, not independent audit
- "Consequential accidents" is a defined term in Railways safety reporting; the 89% fall may not capture all safety incidents
- Investment in tracks alone doesn't capture crowding, station infrastructure, or level-crossing deaths (a major cause of rail fatalities in India)

## Timeline
| Year | Development |
|------|-------------|
| 2014 | Modernisation programme begins; 748 track machines, ~40% of network at 110 kmph+ |
| 2019 | Wider, heavier sleepers adopted to improve stability in summer heat |
| 2025-26 | 1,785 track machines; 80% of network at 110 kmph+; 16 consequential accidents |
| 2026 | USFD testing: 36.2 lakh track km + 2.25 crore welds checked |
