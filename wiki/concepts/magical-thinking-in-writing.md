# Magical Thinking in Writing

## Definition
A state of mind in the middle of a novel where the writer's entire world begins to flow into the book. Every conversation heard, news story read, or random occurrence seems divinely ordained and relevant to the work in progress.

## Why It Matters
This psychological state is what makes the middle of a long project possible. It renders "everything possible" and makes "knotty problems of structure" resolve with inspired ease.

## Key Arguments
- **Total Absorption**: The writer "stops being part of their household" and only exists in the book's world.
- **Divinity of Coincidence**: Random events (a heating break-down, a name in an auction) are seen as "in tune" with the unfinished novel.
- **Inspired Resolution**: Knotty problems suddenly seem easy to fix, though Zadie Smith warns that this "inspired ease" can sometimes lead to leaving unnecessary scaffolding in the work.

## Examples
- **John Baguley**: Smith's anecdote about winning a name in an auction and later finding the same name on her Victorian boiler.
- **The Epigraph**: Picking a random book off the shelf and finding the perfect opening line.
