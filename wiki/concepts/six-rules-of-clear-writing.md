# Six Rules of Clear Writing

**Definition:** George Orwell's six practical rules for clear prose, drawn from "Politics and the English Language" (1946). These are not rules of grammar or elegance — they are rules of *honesty*. Their purpose is to prevent language from doing your thinking for you.

**The Six Rules:**

1. **Never use a metaphor, simile, or figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.**
   → Dead metaphors anesthetize thought. Use only images you have actually visualized.

2. **Never use a long word where a short one will do.**
   → "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence." Length creates false authority.

3. **If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.**
   → Every unnecessary word costs the reader attention. Respect the reader's time.

4. **Never use the passive where you can use the active.**
   → Passive voice conceals agency. "Mistakes were made" — by whom? Active voice demands accountability.

5. **Never use a foreign phrase, scientific word, or jargon if you can think of an everyday equivalent.**
   → Jargon signals in-group membership, not precision. Choose precision.

6. **Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.**
   → The rules serve clarity. If following a rule produces bad writing, break it. Judgment overrides formula.

**Why the Sixth Rule Is the Most Important:**
Rules 1–5 can be taught mechanically. Rule 6 requires wisdom. It is Orwell's acknowledgment that writing cannot be reduced to a checklist — judgment is irreducible. A writer who follows the first five without applying the sixth produces technically correct but lifeless prose.

**The Rules as a Test:**
Orwell suggests a four-question test for every sentence:
- What am I trying to say?
- What words will express it?
- What image or idiom will make it clearer?
- Is this image fresh enough to have an effect?

And two more: Could I put it more shortly? Have I said anything avoidably ugly?

**The Underlying Principle:**
Let meaning choose the word, not the other way around. Start with the thing you want to say; find words that fit it. Do not start with fashionable words and fit your meaning to them.
