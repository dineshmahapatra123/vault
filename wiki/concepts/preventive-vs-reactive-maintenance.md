# Preventive vs Reactive Maintenance

## Definition
Reactive maintenance means fixing things after they break. Preventive maintenance means finding and eliminating problems before failure occurs. The shift between these two modes is not just operational — it changes cost structures, safety profiles, and institutional culture.

## Why It Matters
Infrastructure systems (railways, roads, bridges, electrical grids) are almost always built in reactive-maintenance cultures. The payoff from switching to preventive is asymmetric: you avoid low-probability, high-consequence failures. But the investment is visible and the avoided disaster is invisible — making it politically and organizationally hard to sustain.

## Key Arguments
- **Detection over repair**: Ultrasonic Flaw Detection (USFD) on Indian Railways found hidden rail cracks before fracture — reducing rail and weld failures by 90%. You can't see a hairline crack; you have to look for it systematically.
- **The invisible win problem**: Prevention doesn't produce dramatic results to point to. This is why reactive maintenance is the default: every failure is evidence; every prevented failure is silence.
- **Mechanisation enables prevention**: Manual inspection is too slow and inconsistent to be truly preventive at scale. Machine-based inspection (GPS-enabled OMS, phased-array testing) makes systematic prevention economically viable.

## Examples
1. **Indian Railways USFD testing**: 36.2 lakh track km tested for hidden cracks → consequential accidents fell from 135 (2014-15) to 16 (2025-26)
2. **Aviation maintenance culture**: Commercial aviation shifted to preventive (scheduled overhauls by cycle, not calendar) in the 1970s; transformed the industry's safety record
3. **Software DevOps/SRE**: "Reliability engineering" is preventive maintenance applied to software — building monitoring, alerts, and chaos testing before systems fail in production
