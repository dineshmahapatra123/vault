# Literary Scaffolding

## Definition
The use of external structural frameworks—such as numbering chapters, modeling on other texts, or utilizing a specific rhythm—to provide a sense of security and a goal-oriented endpoint during the construction of a work of fiction.

## Why It Matters
Scaffolding helps manage the "despair" of an endless, unmarked journey. It provides a source of confidence when the writer has none, even if the work would eventually stand without it.

## Key Arguments
- **Temporary Security**: Most scaffolding is only there to make the writer feel secure and is not ultimately necessary for the building to stand.
- **Dismantling Required**: Smith's key advice is to "dismantle it later." Leaving the scaffolding up can make a work feel rigid or artificial.
- **The Facade Strategy**: If the scaffolding must remain, one should "hang a nice facade over it" (decorate it with content) to integrate it into the work.

## Examples
- **Modeling Chapters**: On the books of the prophets, the *Bhagavad Gita*, *Ulysses*, or even 27 speeches by Donald Rumsfeld.
- **Sectional Divisions**: Dividing a novel into fixed chapter counts or sections (e.g., three sections of ten chapters).
