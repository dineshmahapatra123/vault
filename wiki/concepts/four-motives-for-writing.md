# Four Motives for Writing

**Definition:** George Orwell's framework for understanding *why* writers write, drawn from his 1946 essay "Why I Write." He identifies four distinct psychological impulses that drive prose writing — present in all writers, but in different proportions.

**Why It Matters:** This framework cuts through the myth that writers are purely artistic or purely ideological. It insists on the *mixed, honest* nature of creative motivation — and forces any writer to examine their own drives. It's also a framework for reading: understanding a writer's dominant motive explains their choices, their failures, and their integrity.

---

**The Four Motives:**

**1. Sheer Egoism**
Desire to seem clever, to be remembered, to get back at those who underestimated you. Orwell is blunt: *"It is humbug to pretend this is not a motive, and a strong one."* He links this to scientists, politicians, lawyers — anyone in the "top crust of humanity" who refuses to simply dissolve into the mass.

**2. Aesthetic Enthusiasm**
Perception of beauty in words and their right arrangement. The pleasure of a well-turned sentence, the rhythm of a good story, the impact of sound on sound. Even a textbook writer has "pet words and phrases which appeal to him for non-utilitarian reasons." Orwell's own aesthetic anchor: clarity, concreteness, the right word in the right place.

**3. Historical Impulse**
Desire to see things as they are — to find the true facts and record them for posterity. This is the documentary instinct. In *Homage to Catalonia*, Orwell put in a chapter defending falsely accused Trotskyists because *"I happened to know that innocent men were being falsely accused"* — even knowing it would damage the book artistically.

**4. Political Purpose**
*"Using the word 'political' in the widest possible sense."* The desire to push the world in a direction, to change what people think society should look like. Orwell's core claim: *"No book is genuinely free from political bias."* The belief that art has nothing to do with politics is itself a political position.

---

**How They War Against Each Other:**
The four motives don't coexist peacefully. The historical impulse (tell the truth) can clash with aesthetic enthusiasm (make it beautiful). Political purpose can corrupt into propaganda, killing aesthetic integrity. Egoism can distort what "truth" gets told.

Orwell's lifelong project was to *reconcile* these: to make political writing that was also art. He believed it was possible but admitted it was never easy.

**Examples:**
- A journalist driven purely by egoism produces clever prose but skips uncomfortable facts
- A political activist driven purely by purpose produces propaganda — correct but unreadable
- Orwell's *Animal Farm*: his first attempt to fuse all four into one whole, with conscious intention
