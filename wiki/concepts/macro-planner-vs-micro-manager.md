# Macro-Planner vs. Micro-Manager

## Definition
A binary framework proposed by [[zadie-smith]] to describe two distinct methods of novelistic construction. **Macro-Planners** design the entire structure of the work before writing (using notes, outlines, and post-its), while **Micro-Managers** build the work sentence by sentence, floor by floor, without a pre-defined map.

## Why It Matters
This framework provides a vocabulary for writers to understand their own internal process and helps demystify the "craft" of fiction by reducing it to personal temperament and idiosyncratic behavior.

## Key Arguments
- **Macro-Planners**: Have "structural security" from day one. Their work involves moving "internal furniture" (rearranging chapters, changing titles) because the house is already built.
- **Micro-Managers**: Build "floor by floor." Each sentence must be perfectly decorated before the next can be laid. If the first 20 pages aren't right, the whole house might collapse.
- **Mutual Incomprehensibility**: Writers of one type often find the methods of the other "horrifying" or "incomprehensible."

## Examples
- **Zadie Smith**: A self-professed Micro-Manager who spent two years on the first twenty pages of *On Beauty*.
- **The Architect vs. Gardener**: A similar analogy popularized by George R.R. Martin (Macro-Planner = Architect; Micro-Manager = Gardener).
