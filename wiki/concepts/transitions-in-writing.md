# Transitions in Writing

**Definition:** The connective tissue between sentences, paragraphs, and sections — words and phrases that show the reader how two ideas are related, how the argument has moved, and where it is going. David Foster Wallace argued that transitions are perhaps the most neglected and most important craft element in argumentative writing.

**Why It Matters:** Without transitions, the reader must do the work of connecting ideas themselves. They are reading, not following. DFW: *"You are upping the amount of effort the reader has to make in order — forget appreciate — simply to understand what it is that you are communicating."* When that effort becomes conscious, the writing has failed.

**DFW's Core Claim:**
> "I spend a disproportionate amount of my time teaching the students how to write transitions, even as simple ones as 'however' and 'moreover' between sentences. Because part of their belief that the reader can somehow read their mind is their failure to see that the reader needs help understanding how two sentences are connected to each other."

Students fail at transitions not because they are lazy but because they believe the connection is *obvious* — that the reader can read their mind. The reader cannot.

**What Good Transitions Do:**
1. Signal the *logical relationship* between ideas (contrast, addition, cause, consequence, concession)
2. Remind the reader of the *stakes* — why they are still reading
3. Make the argument feel like an *organic whole* rather than a checklist of points

**DFW's Model: Orwell's "Politics and the English Language":**
> "If you look at how that's put together, there's a transition in almost every single paragraph. 'Moreover, not only is this offense common, but it is harmful in this way.' You know where he is in the argument, but you never get the sense that he's ticking off items on a checklist; it's part of an organic whole."

Orwell is DFW's gold standard for argumentative writing precisely because of his use of transitions. This creates a direct link between [[six-rules-of-clear-writing]] and transition craft.

**The Cognitive Science Behind It:**
DFW grounds this in reading psychology: *"Reading is a very strange thing... if you think about what's going on when you read, you're processing information at an incredible rate."* Punctuation, transitions, and sentence structure are not stylistic preferences — they are cognitive guides that help the reader's brain organize information at speed.

The word "flow" and "effortless" — readers use these to describe great writing. They mean not that the writer worked easily, but that *reading* required no effort. Transitions are what create this effect.

**The Draft Method (DFW's Own):**
For argumentative writing, he suggests: write one full draft focused only on the argument (tick the checklist). Then in a subsequent draft, *weave it* — write the transitions that transform the checklist into an organic argument.
