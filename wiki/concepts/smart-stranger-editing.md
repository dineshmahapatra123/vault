# Smart-Stranger Editing

## Definition
The practice of putting a finished manuscript in a drawer for a significant period of time (e.g., three months to a year) to gain the critical distance of an outsider who can see the work for what it is, rather than what the writer intended it to be.

## Why It Matters
A writer in the "thick of it" is often unable to see redundant phrases, show-off metaphors, or "dead wood." The only way to edit effectively is to become the work's reader instead of its writer.

## Key Arguments
- **Critical Distance**: You need the "head of a smart stranger who picks it off a bookshelf."
- **The "Two-Year" Window**: Smith notes that the perfect time to edit a novel is often two years after it's published, usually "ten minutes before you go on stage at a literary festival."
- **Professional Limits**: Even professional editors lose this distance after multiple reads; the creator must find it themselves.

## Examples
- **Alan Hollinghurst**: Spent five years "tinkering" with *The Line of Beauty* after letting it sit.
- **Zadie Smith**: Her "24-karat gold-plated piece of advice": *Step away from the vehicle.*
